Jewish houses in Bivolari

Description

The Jewish houses in Bivolari are a testimony to the existence of a Jewish community in this area until the 1960s, but also of the former prosperity of the locality. Bivolari Fair was mostly inhabited of Jews, brought here by the rulers of Moldavia, in the first half of the XIX century, to make shopping centers. The Jews settled on either side of the trade road that connected Iasi to the north Botoşani county and further from Hotin, so that the localities Bivolari (jud. Iaşi) and Ştefăneşti (jud. Botoșani) have become in time the most developed on this route.The Jews of Bivolari concessioned land from the landowners, then they began to open their shops and sell alcoholic beverages, food, clothing and household tools. Also, most carpenters, tailors and the blacksmiths in the area were Jews. Their shops were always full, and people did not lack of nothing, but their affairs began to crumble with the Second World War and then thanks to state monopolies imposed by the communist regime.
